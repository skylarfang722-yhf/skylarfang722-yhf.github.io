Project Overview

This project investigates whether higher COVID-19 case rates led to higher unemployment in 2020. Using cross-country data and statistical modeling, I examined both developed and developing economies to understand how public health crises affect labor markets differently.

Data & Variables

  • Independent Variable: COVID-19 case rate (cases per million)
  • Dependent Variable: Unemployment rate (%)
  • Sample: 30 countries (developed & developing)
  • Time Period: 2020

Methodology

  • Exploratory data analysis (distributions, scatter plots)
  • Correlation analysis
  • Linear regression modeling
  • Group comparison: Developed vs Developing countries
  • Statistical interpretation & economic reasoning

Key Findings

  • COVID case rate and unemployment show a positive correlation.
  • The relationship is stronger in developed economies than in developing economies.
  • Regression models show COVID case rate explains a meaningful share of unemployment variation.

Interpretation & Insights

Developed economies experienced stronger labor market reactions because their service-based economies and formal employment structures were more vulnerable to lockdowns and business closures. Developing economies showed weaker statistical relationships, partly due to informal labor markets and different reporting structures.

This demonstrates how identical shocks can produce different economic outcomes depending on structural conditions.

Limitations & Improvements

  • Does not capture informal employment accurately.
  • Limited by availability and quality of international data.
  • Future work could include time-series modeling and government policy variables.
